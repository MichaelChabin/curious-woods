The Greeks Called Them "Unspeakable"

π

In 500 BCE, the Pythagoreans discovered something deeply disturbing. They could measure the side of a square perfectly—one unit, exactly. But when they tried to measure the diagonal, no fraction worked.

Not 7/5. Not 99/70. Not 1414/1000. Every attempt came close, but nothing captured it exactly. The number √2 simply... escaped.

They called such numbers alogos—unspeakable, or literally "without ratio." Numbers that couldn't be expressed as one whole number divided by another. Numbers that defied measurement itself.
