## Stained Glass & Color Chemistry
*March 2026*

### Stained Glass Story Series — Four-Story Arc
*Developed March 17, 2026 — Story Developer session*

A progression of four stories that maps to the construction tool's capabilities. Each raises a question the next answers, and Story 3 makes the construction tool feel inevitable rather than assigned.

**Story 1: The Accident**
Origins of glass itself. Egyptian beads, Phoenician myths, Roman murky window glass. The wonder: sand plus fire equals something light passes through. Glass is solidified light. Romans had glass windows across Britain — then that knowledge disappeared after the legions left. When monasteries started building in the 7th century they had to import glaziers from Gaul. Bede mentions this for Jarrow. A skill possessed, lost, and reimported through the church.

**Story 2: The Secret of Red**
The ruby glass problem as a puzzle story. Ruby pot-metal glass is too dark for light to pass through. Solution: "flashing" — a thin layer of colored glass fused to clear. The constraint is the story. The color chemistry already in these notes (gold ruby, pot-metal vs. flashed) belongs here — the science and the story are the same thing. Also: Theophilus, the 12th-century German monk who wrote down everything he observed watching glaziers at work. *On Diverse Arts* is the oldest surviving technical manual for stained glass. Knowledge that lived in guilds, passed master to apprentice, nearly secret — and one monk decides to write it down.

**Story 3: The Wall Becomes a Window**
Gothic engineering as the enabling event. Flying buttresses transfer load outward; walls no longer bear weight; walls become glass. Rose windows are Euclidean geometry made structural and luminous simultaneously — built with compass and straightedge on the workshop floor. The same tools the child has. This is where the construction tool connection becomes inevitable: not an analogy, the same instrument. Story ends with the question hanging: *what designs can you make with just circles and lines?*

**Story 4: Gaudí Reverses Everything**
Sagrada Família as a conversation with the medieval tradition. Color mapped to the sun's path — cool blues and greens on the east (morning), warm reds and oranges on the west (afternoon). Clear glass at the top to flood the vaults, richest color at the bottom where you stand and can read it. The solstice phenomenon: twice a year the setting sun enters nearly horizontally and projects the window designs in colored light onto the opposite wall. The building becomes a projector. Gaudí: "Sunshine is the best painter." He also experimented with trichromy — layering yellow, cyan, magenta glass to create depth — which connects to the RGB interactives (same subtractive color mixing, different medium).

### Connections to Other Material
- Story 3 raises "what designs with circles and lines?" — Islamic geometric art answers it differently and is a natural next series (tiling, symmetry, Islamic patterns)
- Gaudí's trichromy experiment connects to the RGB tools already built
- Persistence of Vision (below) connects to stained glass through the experience of colored light — both are stories about what happens between light and the eye. Not the same series but a natural later pairing.

### Open Questions — Stained Glass Series
*These are genuine open questions for future Story Developer sessions — flagged March 19, 2026*

- **Entry point:** Which story is the right entry point? Chronological (Story 1 first) or most dramatic first and circle back? Affects onboarding experience significantly.
- **Self-containment:** How self-contained does each story need to be? Can Story 3 work for a child who hasn’t read 1 and 2? Determines whether the series must be read in order.
- **Maya handoff:** How does Maya introduce the construction tool at the end of Story 3? This is the critical seam between story and interactive. Needs a joint Story Developer + Maya Design conversation when Maya integration is closer.

### Gold Ruby Glass
Gold cannot be added directly to glass batch as metal — it must first be dissolved in aqua regia (producing chloroauric acid, HAuCl₄). During the melt, gold ions precipitate back out as colloidal nanoparticles at precisely the nanometer scale that scatters red light. Reheating ("striking") develops the color. This is why the process is unique to gold.

### Other Chartres Colorants
Most medieval colorants were metal oxides added directly as powders — no acid dissolution needed:

- **Cobalt** (deep blue) — cobalt-rich smalt from Saxony/Bohemia, or cobalt oxide. Dissolves readily into molten glass.
- **Manganese** (purple/violet) — pyrolusite (MnO₂), ground mineral. Also used as decolorizer against iron green.
- **Copper** (green/turquoise) — copper scale from smithing, or malachite/azurite. Can also produce red under reducing conditions, similar in mechanism to gold ruby but less stable.
- **Iron** (green/amber/brown) — largely unavoidable impurity in the sand itself; medieval glassmakers worked with it.
- Manganese + iron combinations → browns and ambers.

### Pot-metal vs. Flashed Glass
- **Pot-metal**: color throughout the body (oxide additions to batch)
- **Flashed**: thin layer of colored glass over clear — used for red because deeply colored pot-metal red was nearly opaque. Much Chartres ruby is flashed; gold concentration in the colored layer is high relative to thickness.

## Persistence of Vision
*March 2026*

### The Central Mystery
The eye lies; the brain helps it lie. A movie is really 24 still frames. A sparkler trail isn't there. The fan blade has disappeared. This is the hook — and it's genuinely strange.

### Key Phenomena
- **Afterimages** — stare at red, look at white, see cyan. Cone fatigue; complementary color channels. Child's eye is the instrument — the experiment runs inside their head. Highest payoff interactive.
- **Phi phenomenon** — two alternating dots look like one moving dot. The brain interpolates motion that isn't there. Cinema lives here.
- **Troxler effect** — fixate on a point; peripheral colored blob slowly fades, snaps back on eye movement. The brain edits out what it decides is unimportant. Quietly alarming.
- **Flicker fusion** — at what frequency does flashing become steady? Slider interactive. Varies by intensity, retinal position (rods vs. cones), fatigue.

### Vocabulary That Arises Naturally
- *Retina* — where cones live and fatigue
- *Cones and rods* — color/detail center vs. motion/dim-light periphery; explains why Troxler is strongest in peripheral vision
- *Neural adaptation* — the cell slows its firing; the signal fades; the brain fills in
- *Visual cortex* — not receiving but *constructing*; phi phenomenon makes this concrete (motion assembled in cortex, not eye)
- *Opponent process* — red/cyan, blue/yellow, black/white pairs; explains afterimage colors precisely

### Deeper Idea
**You don't see the world; you see your brain's model of the world, updated by the world.** The eye is a sampler, not a camera. Vertiginous when it lands. Opens curiosity rather than closing it — because every other sense is probably doing the same thing.

### Story Arc Possibility
A child notices a filmstrip in an old projector, or a shutter in a camera, and asks *why 24 frames?* That question unravels the whole thing: cone fatigue → flicker fusion → motion interpolation → visual cortex constructing experience rather than recording it.

### Interactive Palette
- **Afterimage generator** — colored shape + timer + blank field. Simplest, highest payoff.
- **Phi phenomenon demonstrator** — two-dot alternation with speed control
- **Flicker fusion slider** — find your personal threshold
- **Troxler fader** — fixation cross + peripheral blob, patience required
- **Complementary color predictor** — "what color will you see?" before the reveal
