The truth is, the Necker Cube doesn't fool your brain all.
The truth is, your brain is doing exactly the right thing:
That's because there are two ways to see this cube

The brain cells you think with are called *neurons.*
Here is what they do when you look at this cube:

First, neurons at the back of your eye, 
register light from the cube and transfer it to the back of the brain,

That information is sent to another set of neurons. 
These organize edges and angles into the idea of a cube...
... except there is a problem with this particular cube.

There is only one cube, 
but it can *mean* two different things.

So your brain does a very clever thing.
It makes copies of both possible cubes. 
Then it shows them to you, one after another.

If you think about it, this is astounding.
It isn't easy. 
Altogether it took as many as 10 to 20 million neurons 
to recognize and remember and let you see those images
one after another. 
Even more amazing, those neurons do all that 
in less than 200 milliseconds...
two tenths of a second...
about the time it takes you to blink.

That isn't all.

### Early colored glass
The earliest stained-glass fragments from the 600s already used several colors that later became standard in medieval windows. Archaeological finds from early churches in Britain and Italy show that glassmakers could reliably produce:
- **Blue** – usually from cobalt
- **Green** – from iron impurities or added copper
- **Amber/yellow-brown** – from iron and sulfur compounds
- **Purple or violet** – from manganese
- **Colorless or pale green glass** iron in sand
These early pieces were mostly **simple colored panes**, not the complex pictorial glass seen later.

The palette used in Gothic windows like those in [Chartres Cathedral](chatgpt://generic-entity?number=0) was largely in place by the **11th–12th centuries**, though it became much more refined. Medieval glassmakers used what is called **pot-metal glass**: the coloring element was mixed directly into the molten glass, so the color ran through the entire thickness.

Typical color chemistry in the Chartres period (early 1200s):
- **Deep blue** – cobalt oxide (the famous “Chartres blue”)
- **Red** – copper or copper-ruby glass; often made as **flashed glass** (a thin red layer over clear glass because solid red absorbs too much light)
- **Green** – iron and copper
- **Purple/violet** – manganese
- **Yellow stain** – silver compounds fired onto the surface (this technique appears slightly later, around the **early 1300s**)
- **White or pale glass** – low-iron glass used as a base for painted details

So the most plausible historical chain is something like:
- **Roman Empire (1st–4th centuries):** colored glass widely produced; windows sometimes glazed.
- **Late Roman / Byzantine world (4th–6th centuries):** colored glass likely begins to be assembled into decorative window panels.
- **6th–7th centuries** – earliest colored window fragments in churches.
- **11th–12th centuries** – stable palette used in Romanesque and Gothic windows.
- **~1200 (Chartres)** – peak development of saturated pot-metal colors, especially blues and reds.
- **~1300** – introduction of **silver stain**, adding luminous yellows and expanding the palette.
One interesting point: the famous **Chartres blue** was not a single secret formula. It results from a particular balance of **cobalt concentration, iron impurities, and glass thickness**, combined with the slightly uneven surfaces of hand-blown glass. That combination produces the deep luminous effect people remember when standing inside the cathedral.
### Glass in Chartres:
How it was made:
- Blown in tubes roughly 1 to 1.5 m long and 30 cm in diameter. These  were then split and rolled flat. 
	1. **A long bubble was blown** on the end of the blowpipe and shaped into a **cylinder** rather than a sphere.
	2. The ends of the cylinder were **cut off**, leaving a hollow glass tube.
	3. A **long slit was cut along the length** of the tube.
	4. The tube was placed in an oven and **reheated**, where it **relaxed and opened into a flat sheet**.
	5. While still warm, it was **flattened with wooden tools or paddles** and allowed to cool.

### Catagories:
- Simple, interesting constructions Nested regular polygons continuing the recursion (triangle inside triangle inside...), the Vesica Piscis and its offspring, the construction of a regular pentagon from scratch (which is genuinely hard and beautiful), the nine-point circle, spirals built from golden rectangles. These are all exact coordinates, easy to generate.
- **Proportion and ratio** — the geometric mean construction (a rectangle with a square cut off, leaving a similar rectangle), the harmonic series as nested rectangles, continued fractions as nested squares. These connect arithmetic to shape in ways that feel like revelation.
- **Optical illusions** — this is a fascinating category because the _geometry_ of illusions is often simple and exact. The Müller-Lyer lines, the Café Wall pattern, the Penrose impossible triangle (which is constructible as a flat SVG in a specific way). The child sees something that "shouldn't" work, which is a different kind of curiosity than construction invites.
- **Proofs without words** — this is perhaps the richest vein. The Pythagorean theorem has a dozen beautiful visual proofs, each a different overlay. The sum of odd numbers making perfect squares (1, 1+3, 1+3+5...) as an L-shaped gnomon pattern. The area of a circle as a rearranged triangle. Galileo's odd-number law for falling bodies. These don't _explain_ the proof — they make the child feel it before they can say it.
- **Curve families** — a parabola as the envelope of straight lines (all constructible!), an ellipse from the trammel construction, a cardioid from circle rolling on circle. These are spectacular because the child can construct the lines and watch the curve emerge from intersections.

### Story Skill
A "storytelling skill" in this context means a document that a Story Developer chat reads and immediately knows how to write a Curious Woods story well — the same way the Layer-1-Build-Brief let a coder build the entire construction engine in one session.

### Elements to specify:

**The voice.** Curious Woods stories are not educational content dressed up as narrative. They're genuine stories that happen to lead somewhere mathematical. The voice should feel like a knowledgeable adult talking to an intelligent child — not simplified, not condescending, not enthusiastic in that performed way educational content tends to be. Eileen's astronomer sensibility is a good reference point: someone who finds things genuinely interesting and assumes you will too.

**The five-page structure.** WHAT → WHERE → WHEN → STORY → WHY. Each page has a job. WHAT orients. WHERE places the child in a world. WHEN gives historical texture. STORY is where something happens — a person, a problem, a discovery, a mystery. WHY is the bridge to the interactive. The skill document needs to say what each page is _for_, not just what it's called.

**The left panel relationship.** Images and animations arrive in their natural narrative moment — the story doesn't pause for them, they accompany it. A story developer needs to think about what's visible in the left panel at each moment, not just what's written in the right panel. This is a different kind of writing than prose alone.

**The Art Lab invitation.** Some stories end with an opening, not a conclusion. The skill should say when that's appropriate and what the invitation sounds like.

**What to avoid.** This might be the most important section. No explaining. No "did you know." No rhetorical questions that telegraph the answer. No summarizing what the child just read. The story trusts the child to have been paying attention.

**A worked example.** The Stained Glass story is already in development — that's the natural first example. Once it exists, future story developers can read it alongside the skill and immediately understand the standard.
