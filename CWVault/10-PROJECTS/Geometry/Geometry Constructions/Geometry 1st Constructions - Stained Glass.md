A first geometry app in which kids create stained glass patterns using Euclidian circles and lines. The app includes a "Share" button that allows them to share their work with grandparents, friends, etc.

**1.The question is, what can kids do to become familiar with the tools prior to making more formal geometric construction. The goal is fascility with the tools.**
Think in terms of _gesture families_ rather than goals. Each family trains a small set of motor, perceptual, and geometric habits while reliably producing attractive results.

**A. Pure compass fluency (no correctness target)**
Goal: comfort, rhythm, trust in circles.

Exercises:
- Repeated circles from a single center, different radii, overlapping freely.
- “Walk the compass”: pick a radius and step it along a line or arc, creating chains of intersections.
- Circle fields: every new circle must be centered on an existing intersection.

What emerges visually:
- Rosette patterns, sixfold symmetry, flower-of-life–like structures.
- Immediate stained-glass potential when enclosed regions are filled.

What is learned implicitly:
- How radius consistency matters.
- That intersections are meaningful objects.
- That geometry _accumulates_ rather than resets.

This stage can last days. No straightedge is even needed at first.

**B. Line as response (straightedge introduced late)**
Goal: lines are reactions to circles, not primary actors.

Exercises:
- Draw a line only _after_ two circles intersect, and only through those intersection points.
- Extend an existing line only if it hits a new intersection.
- Horizontal-only constraint early on (as you suggested) to stabilize orientation.

What emerges:
- Triangular grids, hexagonal meshes, layered equilateral triangles.
- Strong visual coherence even with “random” choices.

What is learned:
- Lines are relationships, not strokes.
- Extension feels natural, not abstract.
- The child begins to “see” triangles before naming them.

**C. Edge subdivision without naming bisection**
Goal: repeated action before formal reasoning.

Exercises:
- “Split this edge again” using circles only (no mention of midpoint).
- Divide triangle edges repeatedly and connect corresponding points horizontally.
- Allow only reuse of the same radius for a while, then allow changes.

What emerges:
- Nested triangles, lattices of smaller equilateral forms.
- Clear scale hierarchy—large → medium → small.

What is learned:
- Proportion before ratio.
- Consistency of construction methods.
- That complexity comes from repetition, not cleverness.

**D. Fill as reward (art-first, math as residue)**
Goal: close the loop with beauty.

Exercises:
- Any closed region may be filled with color.
- Optional palette rules: “no adjacent regions same color,” or “color by size,” or “color by depth (generation).”
- Allow erasing color without erasing geometry.

What emerges:
- Stained-glass designs, Islamic tiling–like patterns, mosaics.

What is learned:
- Closure matters.
- Structure precedes decoration.
- Geometry _wants_ to be art.

 **2. How to know when a child is ready to move on**

The key is that readiness shows up as _behavioral changes_, not verbal understanding. Here are reliable signals.

**Signal 1: Anticipation**
The child places a circle _where they expect an intersection to appear_, not where one already exists. Rhis indicates an internal model of the tool.

 **Signal 2: Economy**
They begin to reuse existing points and radii instead of creating new ones unnecessarily. This is the birth of proof-like thinking, without language.

**Signal 3: Repair**
They notice when something is “off” (a line slightly misaligned, a pattern broken) and fix it voluntarily. This indicates sensitivity to invariants.

**Signal 4: Intentional variation**
They say (or act as if): “What happens if I change just this one thing?” This is experimental reasoning, not play.

**Signal 5: Explanation-by-gesture**
They explain a pattern to someone else by _reconstructing it_, not describing it verbally. This is a strong marker that construction logic is internalized.

When two or three of these are present consistently, the child is ready for:
• Explicit midpoint and angle bisection
• Constraints involving verticals
• Reasoned constructions (“Can you make this shape again without copying?”)

At that point, “classic” Euclidean constructions no longer feel imposed—they feel like power-ups.


**3. Structuring the series of interactives**
A useful organizing principle is _mode_, not difficulty. Each interactive lives in one mode:
- Circle-only
- Circle + line (reactive)
- Subdivision
- Extension
- Coloring / composition

The app never says “Level 4.” It says something like: _“Today you can also extend lines.”_

Sharing then becomes natural: children share _artifacts_, not answers. The social feedback reinforces tool fluency, not correctness.
