Here is a list of fundamental Euclidian Constructions using points, lines, and circles. Definitions:
	- A Point is the smallest possible unit of space (a proton width, a pixel, etc)
	- Exactly one line can pass  through two points. It can extend arbitrarily
	- Given a point as its center and a second point as its radius, a circle is the set of points that are exactly one radius from the center.
1. Constructions with lines: 
	1. A line can be extended using the two points that define it.  
	2. A line can be bisected using its two points as the center and edges of two circles. Connect the points where those circles intersect to find the midpoint of a line.
	3. A line can be divided into equal lengths using a chain circles of a given radius.

3. **Copy a length** — reproduce a segment by using it as the radius of a circle. Then move the circle. Euclid would not like this, but what he did was equivalent
4. **Construct an equilateral triangle** — use two equal circles on a segment to locate a third point at equal distance from both ends.
5. **Bisect a segment** — locate its midpoint using intersecting equal-radius circles.
6. **Bisect an angle** — use equal arcs to find the symmetry line of the angle.
7. **Construct a perpendicular to a line at a point** — You do this when you construct the equilateral triangle
8. **Construct a parallel through a given point** Through a point not on a given line, construct a perpendicular to the line, then a perpendicular to that perpendicular; the result is parallel to the original line.
9. **Construct a circle from center and radius** — define a locus of points at fixed distance.
10. **Step off equal lengths along a circle** — repeat a chord to divide the circumference.
11. **Inscribe a regular polygon in a circle** — divide the circle into equal arcs, then connect adjacent points.
12. **Construct the golden ratio** — divide a segment in extreme and mean ratio using similar triangles.
13. **Construct similar figures at a given scale** — Use parallel lines to copy angles and proportional lengths, preserving shape while changing size.
