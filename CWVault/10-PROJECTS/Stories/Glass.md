At **20 °C**, nothing visible happens. You just have a pale granular mixture: hard silica grains, white limestone powder, and soda ash crystals.

By **100 °C**, free water is gone. If the batch was damp, steam can escape, but the ingredients themselves are still essentially unchanged. Tin would already have melted by about **232 °C**, and lead by about **327 °C**, so those are nice comparison points on the way up. 

Around **200–400 °C**, the batch may begin to look slightly more settled or caked if there were fine powders or moisture, but you still do not have glass. This is more drying and early solid-state rearrangement than true melting. Copper is still solid here, and remains solid until about **1083 °C**. 

At **573 °C**, something genuinely interesting happens to the sand if it is quartz: quartz changes from **alpha-quartz to beta-quartz**. That is a structural change inside the crystal, not melting, but it matters because it comes with a small expansion and helps explain why silica behaves oddly as it is heated. 

From about **700 to 800 °C**, the batch begins to look more active. The grains start to react at their surfaces. It is still mostly a granular mass, but the soda component is beginning to attack the silica and form the first sodium silicates. This is the stage where the mix starts moving from “powder” toward “partly fused crust.” This is a reasonable inference from the known behavior of soda in silica glasses and from the onset of carbonate decomposition and sodium carbonate melting just above this range. 

Around **825–840 °C**, the limestone begins to decompose: **calcium carbonate breaks down into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide**. This is one of the first major chemical changes in the batch, and it releases gas, so the mixture can look foamy or unsettled. 

At about **851–856 °C**, **sodium carbonate melts**. That is a major turning point, because now you have a true liquid phase in the batch, and that liquid can wet the silica grains and 
By roughly **900 °C**, you can fairly say that the first real glass is beginning to form. Not all the sand is melted; instead, you have patches of newly formed glass plus unreacted silica and bubbling gases. It is a sticky, chemically busy stage rather than a clear liquid. This is an inference based on the sodium carbonate melt and the decomposition of limestone occurring just below this temperature. 

From about **1000 to 1100 °C**, the batch becomes much more visibly fused. More silica dissolves into the alkali-rich melt, and the mass changes from a crusty, foamy mixture into something more uniformly molten. If you had copper metal in the furnace as a comparison, it would melt at about **1083 °C**. 

At **1100–1200 °C**, much of the batch can now be called molten glass, but it is still likely to be bubbly, streaky, and chemically uneven. This is often hot enough to make glass, but not yet hot enough to make especially clear, well-refined glass. Studies of soda-lime-silica melts commonly treat this range as molten, while higher temperatures are used for better fining and homogenization. 

At **1250–1300 °C**, the melt is far more fluid. Bubbles rise more easily, unmelted grains continue dissolving, and the color chemistry becomes more reliable. This is the region where the glass starts to look like something a glassmaker would recognize as a workable melt, though it may still benefit from more heat or time. 

By **1400 °C**, you are in a very good working and refining range for soda-lime-silica glass. At this point the melt is fluid enough to homogenize well, gases can escape more readily, and the batch can become the clear, coherent molten glass you want before gathering or casting. Experimental studies on soda-lime-silica melts commonly use **1400–1500 °C** as an observation and melt-treatment range.

Nothing much happens at first. Water goes. At 573 degrees the quartz in the sand quietly changes its crystal form. Around 830 degrees the limestone begins to break apart and breathe out carbon dioxide. Around 850 degrees the soda ash melts. Around 900 degrees the first real glass begins to appear. By 1100 or 1200 degrees much of the batch is molten, though still bubbly. By 1400 degrees the glass is truly ready to work.”

Green
- Element/oxide: Iron (Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺)
- Typical amount: already present as an impurity; deliberate addition ~0.1–1% iron oxide
- Raw source: iron-rich sand, furnace scale, rust
- Notes:  
	- More reducing (low oxygen) → Fe²⁺ → bluish-green
	- More oxidizing → Fe³⁺ → yellow-green

Blue
- Element/oxide: Cobalt (Co²⁺)
- Typical amount: 0.01–0.1% (very strong colorant)
- Raw source: cobalt-bearing minerals (e.g., cobaltite, smalt)
- Notes:  
	- Works in both oxidizing and reducing melts; reliably intense

Purple
- Element/oxide: Manganese (Mn³⁺)
- Typical amount: 0.1–1%
- Raw source: manganese ores (pyrolusite)
- Notes:  
	1. In small amounts it can decolorize green (counteracts iron)
	2. In larger amounts gives violet/purple
	3. More effective in oxidizing conditions

Red (copper ruby)
• Element/oxide: Copper (Cu⁺ colloids in glass)
• Typical amount: ~0.1–1% copper compounds
• Raw source: copper scale, bronze scrap, malachite
• Notes:
	• Needs a reducing melt and careful cooling (“striking”) to form red particles
	• Too much oxygen → green/blue instead

Blue-green / Turquoise (copper)
- Element/oxide: Copper (Cu²⁺)
- Typical amount: 0.5–2%
- Raw source: same as above
- Notes:  
	- Oxidizing conditions → blue-green tones

Yellow (iron or lead-tin)
• Element/oxide:
• Iron (Fe³⁺) for pale yellow
• Lead-tin compounds for stronger opaque yellow
• Typical amount: iron ~0.1–1%; lead-tin higher in special glasses
• Raw source: iron impurities; lead ores + tin ash
• Notes:
	• Oxidizing atmosphere favors yellow from iron

White / Opaque (milk glass)
- Element/oxide: Tin oxide (SnO₂), sometimes antimony compounds
- Typical amount: ~5–10% for strong opacity
- Raw source: tin ash, recycled enamels
- Notes:  
	- Not just color—creates tiny crystals that scatter light

Amber / Brown (iron + sulfur)
• Element/oxide: Iron with sulfur compounds
• Typical amount: small additions; often arises from fuel/ash contamination
• Raw source: ash, impure sands, furnace gases
• Notes:
	• Depends on reducing conditions and sulfur presence
