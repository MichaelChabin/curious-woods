# Reference Atlas — Human Timeline
*12,000 years of positive change*

## How to read the dates

CW years run from 0 (= 10,000 BCE) to ~12,026 (today). This makes the scale usable as a scale — distances between events are visible at a glance, and no subtraction is required.

For dates in the common era, the conventional year is **bolded** within the CW year. The leading `1` is unbolded context (rendered smaller in the beam tool). So 1**1599** reads as **1599** at a glance, with the quiet prefix reminding you how far into civilization we are.

For BCE dates, the full CW year is bold (there is no conventional counterpart a child would recognize), and the BCE date appears in italics at the end of the entry — there for the curious, but not competing for attention.

## Editorial principle

Entries favor environmental change, invention, and the spread of ideas. No wars. No great men. The goal is a set of vivid landmarks a child encounters repeatedly across stories until they become felt reference points — not facts to memorize, but places they recognize.

## The timeline

**0** — End of the last ice age: glaciers a mile thick over Chicago and London melt; sea levels rise 120 meters over the next few millennia *(10,000 BCE)*

**500** — English Channel floods: Britain becomes an island as the North Sea breaks through a land bridge connecting it to Europe *(9,500 BCE)*

**1000** — First permanent villages: people in the Fertile Crescent build houses they intend to live in forever *(9,000 BCE)*

**1000** — Wheat and barley domesticated: wild grasses are selectively grown until their seeds are too heavy to scatter on their own *(9,000 BCE)*

**1000** — Maize domestication begins in Mexico: a wild grass called teosinte, with just a few hard kernels, is slowly coaxed into becoming corn — one of the most transformed plants in history *(9,000 BCE)*

**1500** — Goats and sheep domesticated: animals that follow a leader are coaxed into following humans instead *(8,500 BCE)*

**2000** — The Sahara is green: grasslands, lakes, and rivers cover what is now desert; elephants, hippos, and crocodiles live there *(8,000 BCE)*

**2500** — Cattle domesticated: aurochs — wild cattle larger than modern bison — are slowly tamed over generations *(7,500 BCE)*

**3000** — First fired pottery: clay shaped by hand and hardened in fire makes it possible to store and cook liquid food *(7,000 BCE)*

**3500** — Copper smelting begins: someone discovers that green rocks melted in a fire produce a shiny, shapeable metal *(6,500 BCE)*

**4000** — Irrigation canals in Mesopotamia: farmers dig channels to carry river water to fields far from the bank *(6,000 BCE)*

**4000** — Rice domesticated in China: wild rice along the Yangtze River is cultivated — it will eventually feed more people than any other crop *(6,000 BCE)*

**4500** — The Sahara dries out: a slow shift in Earth's orbit changes rainfall patterns; the grasslands become desert within a few centuries *(5,500 BCE)*

**5000** — Potter's wheel invented in Mesopotamia: a spinning disc shapes clay evenly — the first wheel is a tool for making pots, not for moving things *(5,000 BCE)*

**5000** — Potato domesticated in the Andes: high in the mountains, people cultivate wild potatoes — a crop that will eventually feed more of Europe than wheat *(5,000 BCE)*

**5500** — Sailing invented in Egypt: a cloth sail catches wind and moves a boat without anyone rowing *(4,500 BCE)*

**6000** — Bronze discovered: mixing copper and tin makes a harder metal than either alone *(4,000 BCE)*

**6000** — Cacao cultivated in Mesoamerica: the cacao tree is tended for its seeds, which are fermented, dried, and made into a bitter ceremonial drink *(4,000 BCE)*

**6500** — Wheel used for transport: wheels go under a cart; oxen can now haul loads that would take dozens of people *(3,500 BCE)*

**6800** — Writing invented in Sumer: merchants press symbols into clay tablets to track grain and animals — the first written records *(3,200 BCE)*

**6800** — Writing invented independently in Mesoamerica: Zapotec people in what is now Mexico develop a writing system with no connection to Sumerian — a second invention of writing *(3,200 BCE)*

**7000** — Egyptian hieroglyphics: a third writing system develops independently, using pictures that gradually become sounds *(3,000 BCE)*

**7500** — Indus Valley cities: Mohenjo-daro is built with straight streets, indoor plumbing, and standardized brick sizes — the most carefully planned cities of the ancient world *(2,500 BCE)*

**8000** — Cast iron invented in China: China discovers how to make iron fluid enough to pour into molds — Europe won't manage this for another 2,000 years *(2,000 BCE)*

**8500** — Alphabet invented in Phoenicia: a writing system of ~22 sounds that can spell any word replaces systems with hundreds of symbols *(1,500 BCE)*

**8800** — Iron smelting spreads widely: iron ore is common nearly everywhere; the knowledge to smelt it spreads across three continents *(1,200 BCE)*

**9400** — Coins invented in Lydia (now Turkey): guaranteed weights of metal are stamped — money becomes portable and universal *(600 BCE)*

**9500** — Decimal system with zero developed in India: a positional number system including zero makes calculation vastly more powerful *(500 BCE)*

**9700** — Woodblock printing invented in China: carved wooden blocks pressed in ink reproduce text and images — more than a thousand years before Gutenberg *(300 BCE)*

**9800** — Paper invented in China: bark and hemp fibers are pulped, spread thin, and dried — writing no longer requires clay, stone, or animal skin *(200 BCE)*

1**0050** — Glassblowing spreads: a pipe and a breath makes a glass vessel in minutes; glass moves from luxury to everyday object

1**0700** — Indian decimal notation carried westward by Arab mathematicians, replacing Roman numerals across Europe

1**0900** — Windmills spread from Persia into Europe: wind turns millstones; grain can be ground without a river nearby

1**1000** — Norse reach North America: people from Scandinavia reach Newfoundland — the two halves of the world briefly touch

1**1200** — Movable type invented in China, refined in Korea: individual characters cast in metal can be rearranged to print any text — centuries before Europe

1**1440** — Gutenberg's printing press in Europe: books that took a monk a year to copy are printed in a day

1**1590** — First microscope in Delft: lens grinders in the Netherlands discover that stacked lenses make small things visible

1**1599** — Shakespeare writes Hamlet, Julius Caesar, As You Like It, and Henry V in a single year in London

1**1700** — Steam power harnessed: coal heated to steam pushes a piston — the first engine not powered by muscle, wind, or water

1**1800** — Vaccination discovered: milkmaids who catch cowpox don't get smallpox — the first hint that mild disease can prevent fatal disease

1**1850** — Germ theory established: disease is caused by living organisms too small to see, not by bad air or imbalance of humors

1**1950** — Structure of DNA discovered: the molecule that carries instructions for building every living thing is found to be a double helix — life has a readable code

1**2000** — Human genome mapped: the complete set of instructions for building a human being is read for the first time
