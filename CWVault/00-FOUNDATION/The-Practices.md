# The Practices

## What practice means in this project

When we say *practice* in this project, we do not mean drill.

We mean something older and better. In the Zen tradition there is the idea of effortless practice — a returning, rhythmic engagement with something that, over time, becomes continuous with the practitioner. The monk sweeping the courtyard is not doing repetitions. He is sweeping, and the sweeping has become part of him.

Children do this naturally. An infant does not memorize where the kitchen is. She moves through the kitchen, at different times, from different angles, with attention, and *comes to know* it through a process that is beautifully efficient and genuinely not fully understood. The knowing is perceptual and structural, not propositional. She could not tell you where the refrigerator is. She can just go to it.

Every mammal learns this way. It is, at some level, what brains are for. And most schooling fights against it rather than working with it — replacing *explore and come to know* with *be told and try to retain*. The result is that children come to believe learning is effortful and unpleasant, when in fact the most powerful learning they ever did — language, space, faces, their own bodies — happened without any of that.

The platform's approach to practice is built on a simple commitment: **the modes of practice available to the child should resemble the way children already learn, not the way schools teach.**

## Why practice at all

The human brain learns from virtually any experience. Connections are made unconsciously and effortlessly. But memory is expensive. The brain cannot maintain everything it encounters. Information that is used, revisited, or meaningfully connected to existing knowledge gets stabilized; the rest is rapidly deprioritized. Within hours or days, it becomes difficult or impossible to retrieve.

To remember something for long, we need to return to it. That return is what practice is. Done well, it is not a burden. It is how something becomes part of you.

Thanks largely to professional athletes and musicians, we know a lot about what *done well* means. We know that practice works best when the practitioner chose the material. We know that the timing of returns has a powerful influence on retention. And we know that *how* one practices matters — some methods are much more effective than others, and different methods suit different kinds of material.

Effective practice for a child, then, maximizes her choice, is scheduled strategically, and uses methods tuned both to her and to what she is trying to learn.

## Three principles

Before describing the forms, three principles hold across all of them.

**Choice.** The child chooses what to practice. She does not practice what the platform decides she should. Children remember what they care about — what interests them, what surprises them, what they find funny or useful or beautiful. The neuroscience is substantial: dopamine and norepinephrine, released during engagement, tag memories as worth keeping. Caring is not decoration; it is the mechanism by which the memory becomes durable.

To support this, every story and puzzle and interactive contains a button labeled **I Want to Remember This**. When the child presses it, the linked content joins her practice list. She can also ask Maya to add things — entire stories, a poem, a set of images, whatever she wants to keep.

This commitment has a consequence that runs back through the rest of the platform. For a child to choose a story, the story must be worth choosing. For her to choose a practice tool, the tool must engage her fully and convince her it works. The commitment to child choice is a design constraint on everything upstream.

**Scheduling.** Memory loss follows roughly the shape Ebbinghaus described in the 1880s — rapid at first, slow after — but well-timed returns flatten the curve dramatically. The best schedule varies from child to child and from material to material. Maya understands how to analyze a child's pattern, build an effective schedule, and keep it updated as the child progresses. (See Appendix I for the science.)

But the schedule is not a leash. Maya holds it; the child opens her practice list when she wants to. If Maya notices that a good return-moment has arrived for a particular item, she may surface it quietly on the dashboard. The child decides whether to engage.

**Method matches material.** Different kinds of material want different kinds of practice. You can't learn to recognize numerical patterns by writing essays; you can't remember the story of ancient glass by clicking buttons in rhythm. Part of the craft is knowing which form suits what.

There is a second reason method matters especially for children. Focused practice places heavy demands on cognitive control systems that fatigue more quickly in young minds than in older ones. When a child practices too long in one mode, attention becomes unstable, errors increase, and thinking gets foggy. A common and damaging result is that the child concludes she is *not good at* whatever she was working on. In fact the child is fine; the method or the duration was wrong. Simply switching to a different form of practice often resolves this entirely. Maya watches for these moments.

### Writing to Remember

*For coherent bodies of material the child cares about keeping whole.*

This practice transforms loosely connected material that is hard to remember into a coherent story that can be recalled as a whole. Cognitive scientists might call this *elaborative encoding*. It is especially useful when a child wants to remember a suite of related material — how plants made the transition to land, why Shakespeare and his colleagues had to steal the Globe Theater, what happened at Gare Montparnasse in 1895.

The idea is simple. When we read a story, we tend to remember isolated facts that interest us. Because they are isolated, they are rarely used, rarely reinforced, and easily forgotten.

That is very different from what happens when we *write* a story. Creating a story means collecting information we care about, organizing it into a coherent and entertaining whole, and giving the result a title. As we do that, the brain builds and reinforces a single neural structure composed of the linked ideas and everything we associate with them. Each revision strengthens those links. That is why this practice produces durable, useful memory.

To make it possible, the child needs the help of a patient and knowledgeable editor. That role is Maya's.

**A worked example.** Imagine the child has just completed a long story on ancient glass, from the earliest examples to the development of glass-blowing in Rome around 100 BCE. She liked it. She asked Maya to help her remember it.

The first challenge is to find out what, exactly, she wants to remember. One way to begin is to have her tell the story aloud while Maya records the audio and transcribes it. The first version might sound like this:

> Okay, there was a Roman who told about lightning striking in the desert because when the lightning hit the sand it melted it into a ball of really hot glass. There's a name for that ball of glass. I don't remember it, but here is a picture of one. Then, when people found that, they wondered if they could make something like it by melting sand. They had furnaces that they used to melt copper and make pottery, but they couldn't get the fire hot enough to melt sand. It has to be really hot. But there was this kind of… salt. It was like baking soda. I don't remember the name of that either. But when they added it to the sand, they could just barely get it to melt.
>
> At first the glass they made looked like shiny stones you'd use for jewelry. It wasn't transparent, but it was valuable and they kept making it. And as they did they figured out how to give it color.
>
> But in Egypt they learned how to do amazing things with glass. They would make a core out of clay, then heat glass rods till they were soft, and wrap them around the core. And they decorated it by melting really thin strings of colored glass on it and dragging that back to make the curled shapes. That's how they made this fish!

This is an excellent start. The details she included are almost certainly the ones she cares about. Better still, the retelling itself has reinforced everything she mentioned. But it is still memory; turning memory into a story is the work of many drafts.

Drafts are never failures. Each is a glorious opportunity to get it right, fill in what's missing, add new insight or a new angle. In the next session, Maya and the child might work in the name of the salt (natron) and what it is made of (sodium carbonate with sodium bicarbonate and some sodium chloride). In another session they might place the elements of the story in context — the first glass was made before the great pyramids were built, around the time the Sahara was drying and turning to sand. Writing didn't exist yet. Egypt was thriving when the glass fish was made. Some surviving Egyptian math textbooks are older than it.

They work on the story on a schedule that is designed to move its elements into long-term memory. The schedule is not rigid. It is also driven by when the child wants to work. If she is fully engaged, new ideas come to her regularly. If she needs time away, the story waits.

Few humans have the time, skill, or breadth to support this kind of work. Maya is exactly for it. This practice gives the child a chance to explore something she cares about as deeply as she likes, for as long as she likes, whenever she likes. She can return to the story after months.

And it does something else at least as valuable. It strengthens the child's ability to write and tell stories. A child who does this seriously across many stories over many years becomes a writer — not by being taught writing, but by doing it, in service of things she cares about.

**Sharing.** A story the child has worked on is not trapped on the device. She can send it to people who would love to read it — a grandmother, a best friend, an aunt across the country. The list of recipients is hers; she enters the emails, and she chooses who sees what. The platform does not publish her work, does not train on it, does not share it anywhere she did not send it. A story shared this way gets read by someone who loves her, and she usually gets a reply. That closing of the loop — *I made this; you saw it; it mattered* — is not incidental to the practice. It is part of why the practice is worth doing. Children do real work when the work is real.

### Procedural Practice

*For skills that want to run below conscious thought.*

Among the most effective ways to practice anything is to repeat an action deliberately and rhythmically, with minimal conscious effort. Golfers do this when they hit hundreds of drives in succession, focusing on the feel of the shot in general rather than any particular aspect of it. Musicians do it when they play scales. It works because repetition allows actions to become smooth, predictable, and almost effortless. As conscious control steps back, timing tightens and error correction becomes automatic.

This is the practice of patterns. Once children learn how to slip into it, they can sustain it comfortably — much longer than any focus-heavy practice. It is an excellent way for their brains to recover from work that has been demanding in other ways.

The method is enormously versatile. What follows is one worked example. The same form adapts to learning Chinese characters, recognizing which algebraic transformations are legal, identifying spectral types of stars, or knowing artists by style.

**A worked example: factoring small numbers.** A number under 1000 appears on the screen. Below it, four buttons show four small primes: 2, 3, 5, 7.

After three seconds, a quiet *tap* sounds. A brief pause. Then one of the buttons highlights — say the 2 — the number is divided by 2, and the result replaces it. Three seconds later another *tap*, another highlight, another division. And so on, until the number is fully factored. The fully factored display rests for three seconds, and then a new number appears.

The child can watch this for as long as she likes. The watching is not waiting; it is the beginning of the practice. She sees the rhythm before she joins it.

When she is ready, she clicks a factor button as close as possible to the moment of the tap. If her click is right — both the correct factor and on time — a soft *ding* sounds and the number divides. If she clicks the wrong factor, or too early, or too late, nothing happens. No error sound. No red flash. No "try again." The platform does not punish the miss. It simply does not advance.

This no-punishment feedback is not cosmetic. It is what allows the practice to enter flow. Punishment recruits the conscious, evaluative part of the mind — the part this practice is trying to let rest. Silence when she misses lets her stay in the rhythm and try again on the next beat.

Rhythm is central, just as it is in shooting free throws or playing scales. It helps the child enter a quiet, flow-like state in which she begins to notice patterns inherent in factoring.

There are many such patterns. Even numbers have a factor of 2; any number ending in 5 or 0 has a factor of 5. Less obvious: there are only 140 numbers under 1000 that are products of 2, 3, 5, and 7 — and 42 of them are already on the multiplication table. After factoring out the 2s and 5s, only 15 possibilities remain, 8 of which are familiar. The other seven — 147, 189, 243, 343, 441, 567, 729 — become familiar to the child very quickly.

That familiarity is what expertise feels like. That familiarity *is* expertise.

A distinction worth naming: children doing this practice do not memorize numbers and their factors. They come to *recognize* them. Numbers and their properties become familiar objects. The child does not figure out that 210 has factors 2, 3, 5, 7; she sees it, the way a fluent reader sees the word *orange* without sounding it out. The recognition happens without conscious thought — the way a golfer adjusts to a breeze without thinking about it.

Adding an 11 button expands the territory by 50 more numbers. Many of them — 121, 242, 363, 484, 616, 737, 858, 979 — carry patterns that are easy to spot, interesting on their own, and clearly link the number to 11. Once the child is fluent with the first four primes, adding 11 does not make anything harder. It makes it more interesting.

To be fair, the child is not technically *factoring* — she is not doing the division. Division requires the focused reasoning that would interrupt the practice. By leaving the division to the tool, the practice frees her brain to notice the *pattern*. Later, when she does do long division (or when a probe story makes her want to), she will come to it with an already-fluent sense of which numbers divide which.

**Rhythm and session length.** Three seconds is a starting pace, not a law. Children differ, and the same child differs from day to day. Maya can tighten the tempo when the child is warm, loosen it when she is just beginning — silently, without making a show of it. Sessions should be short. The research on motor learning and cognitive learning both agree: short, frequent sessions beat long, infrequent ones by a wide margin. Maya should know when a session has reached its good-ending point and let it close cleanly, without any "you did X problems!" summary. That would import the grading frame. A quiet close is correct.

### Inhabiting

*For material whose surface (lines, dates, sequences) is easier to remember when it sits inside a world.*

Actors rarely memorize lines by rote. Instead they use *blocking*: working out where the character stands, whom she speaks to, what she feels, how the scene unfolds in space and time. By building a detailed internal model of the scene — setting, movement, emotion, intention — the lines come along automatically. What is learned is not just the text, but the text inside its meaning and its context. Actors tend to remember their lines for a very long time.

The same form works for children and for many kinds of material. Suppose the child wants to remember Emily Dickinson's "I'm Nobody! Who are you?" Working with Maya, she might ask: Who is speaking? Where are they? Is this a secret being shared or a public announcement? Is the frog ridiculous or proud? Is the poem whispered or proclaimed? The poem becomes a small world rather than a sequence of lines.

Each detail serves two purposes. It signals to the brain that this material is worth keeping. And it creates multiple paths back to the memory. Recall no longer hangs by a single fragile thread; it rests on a web of associations. The result is memory that is both easier to retrieve and far more durable.

This approach works for poems, plays, speeches, historical events, scripts, languages, and any material the child genuinely wants to remember. It is not brute-force memorization; it is *strategic reconstruction*. It aligns with how memory actually works rather than fighting it.

Maya's role here is that of a curious, unhurried companion. She asks the kind of questions that populate the world — never interrogating, never testing, just wondering aloud. *What kind of room is this? What does she smell? Why is she telling him this now?* The child answers, or she doesn't. Over several sessions, the scene fills in. The lines stay, because they now live somewhere.

## A note on what's missing

This catalog is not complete. Other forms will be named as the project learns. Candidates already visible:

- **Retrieval.** The child tries to remember something before checking. Low-stakes self-testing, which the research calls the *testing effect*. Useful for facts embedded in structure. The form is old and well-studied; the care is in keeping it stakes-free.
- **Performance.** Doing the thing in the presence of a witness. Reading a story aloud, playing a piece, demonstrating a new skill. The witness is not evaluating; the witness is *seeing*. Consolidation through being seen is real and distinct from private practice.
- **Conversation.** The child articulates what she knows, in a way that forces the inchoate to take shape. Explaining the solar system to a younger sibling; defending her solution; disagreeing about a character's motive. Articulation is a form of knowing.
- **Returning.** The oldest form. Simply coming back to something, after time has passed, and finding it still there or mostly there. No task, no goal — just revisiting. A favorite book, a half-solved puzzle left on the table, a question asked again six months later. The spacing effect without the word.

Each of these, when we write it, will have its own chapter here.

## How the practices relate to the rest of the platform

Stories, interactives, and Maya all feed the practices.

Any story can generate practice material — via the *I Want to Remember This* button, or by the child asking Maya to help her keep something. A story about the Gare Montparnasse can become a Writing-to-Remember project that runs for months. A story about the Egyptian glass fish becomes an Inhabiting practice in which the child populates the workshop in her imagination until the fish has a life around it.

Any interactive can generate practice material too. A child who has enjoyed a session with the factoring tool may not realize she is already practicing — the tool is designed so that the practice is indistinguishable from the play. But the pattern recognition she is building is the same pattern recognition a child in a drill session is supposed to acquire, and she is acquiring it more deeply, because she chose it and she is in flow.

Maya sits across all of this. She holds the schedule. She notices when the child is drifting out of flow and gently suggests switching forms — or stopping. She never gates. She never grades. She is not a tutor; she is the patient companion whose job is to make the practice possible and then stay out of the way.

## Appendix I — The science of spacing and the forgetting curve

In the 1880s the German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus studied memory by memorizing lists of nonsense syllables — *taz, bik, lop* — chosen precisely because they had no meaning. By measuring how quickly he forgot them, he discovered what we now call the *forgetting curve*: memory fades rapidly at first, with most loss occurring within hours or days, followed by a long slow tail in which some traces persist much longer.

Modern neuroscience agrees with the overall shape but refines it in important ways. There is no single forgetting curve. Different kinds of memory decay at different rates, and forgetting itself is not a flaw but an active, necessary process. The brain must discard unused information to remain flexible. What matters is not whether forgetting happens, but what survives it.

Several factors reshape the curve dramatically.

- **Spacing.** Rehearsal spaced over days or weeks flattens the curve. Each well-timed act of recall reconstructs the memory and makes it more stable.
- **Emotion, meaning, and curiosity** warp the curve as well. We reliably remember what we care about, what surprises us, and what we wanted to know before we learned it.
- **Connection.** Memories that are richly connected — to stories, images, or prior knowledge — last far longer than isolated facts.

Different memory systems behave differently. *Procedural* memories (skills) show very little decay once established. *Semantic* memories (facts) decay more readily but respond strongly to spaced practice. *Episodic* memories (stories and events) are often remarkably durable, especially when emotionally tagged. *Visual* memory varies widely, depending on attention and expertise.

The implication is clear: **method matters far more than effort.**

We now have predictive models that can estimate an individual learner's forgetting curve. This makes it possible to design personalized rehearsal schedules that are much more efficient than uniform repetition. Maya uses such a model. When a child wants to remember something, the schedule is not generic; it adapts to her, and it updates as she progresses.

One powerful illustration of strategic rehearsal is the actor's use of blocking — which is why we promoted it to its own form above (*Inhabiting*). Inhabiting is not a memory trick. It is what the research predicts: material embedded in a dense web of meaning and association is far more durable than material carried on a thin thread of rote.

The forms in this document are not folk wisdom. They are built to align with what the research on memory actually says. The alignment is what makes them work.
